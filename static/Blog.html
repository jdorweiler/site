Saving my old desk lamp

May 3, 2012 by jdorweiler
I have this old desk light that’s great for doing small electronics work. It uses a circular fluorescent light and through the center of the lamp is a big magnifying lens. Unfortunately it fell off my desk the other day and broke the bulb.

Instead of tossing it I though I would bring it back to life as in LED desk lamp. All I needed was a new power source and some way to hold the LEDs in place. I remembered seeing something perfect for this project at the nearby surplus store. They had these cheap AC to USB converters that put out 5V and 0.7A. Perfect for powering a bunch of LEDs.

I designed a 30 LED holder using some CAD software and sent it to my 3D printer.

Printed and ready for soldering.

I cracked open the power converter. It looks like a pretty typical switching power supply such as this one. The power off the mains goes through a full wave rectifier and into some control IC which I can’t read the part number on. Then into a transformer to bring it down to 5.0V. It appears this PSU has no feedback loop as it doesn’t seem to be an optocoupler for a control signal.
I soldered in all the LEDs to the holder which fit perfectly into the spot where the florescent bulb use to be. Powered it up and it’s bright, very bright.



Full wave rectifier

April 30, 2012 by jdorweiler

I has someone ask me about AC power and how to get a constant DC supply out of it. I though I’d do a quick write up on how a bridge rectifier works and get a little bit more experience modeling circuits in the process. For this example I’m showing the full wave rectifier. There’s also something called a half wave rectifier but the idea is essentially the same. The AC supply that comes out of your outlet in your house is 120V and 60Hz (in the US at least). Here’s a graph of what the voltage looks like that looks like over time. The time scale on this graph is 100ms or 0.1sec.

This graph shows the voltage varying from +120V to -120V in a sine wave just like it should be. The time between peaks on the graph is 16.66ms which will give you exactly 60 peaks per second (or 60Hz). In order to get a DC voltage from this we can use a bridge rectifier. This uses four diodes that are arranged in what’s called a diode bridge. One easy way to think of it is as a device that take the negateive portion of the sine wave and flips it over to be positive. Here’s a schematic of a full wave rectifier using a diode bridge connected to a 120V 60Hz source. This is what the output voltage (Vout) will look like with this circuit.
Notice that the negative portion of the original sine wave is now flipped over. You can see this in the time between peaks (now 8.33ms). A good start but this would make a pretty poor power supply. The voltage now varies from nearly 0V to 120V. A good way to take care of this problem is to connect a capacitor across Vout and ground.


This gives the circuit a way to store all that voltage. Vout with this circuit looks like this;



In this graph the blue and red waves show the two phases in the input voltage. And green is Vout. As each of the input waves increases you can see the Vout increasing and charging the capacitor. Then as the input voltage drops off the capacitor starts discharging until the next input wave recharges it again. This makes for a much smoother output voltage. A neat thing to see from this graph is that the capacitor is actually supplying the voltage a majority of the time. There’s a small ripple voltage of about 10V but that’s much better than a 120V ripple. This 10V ripple can be further filtered but I’ll that for another post.



Adjustable voltage supply

April 24, 2012 by jdorweiler
This is a current larger-scale electronics project. For this part of the project, I needed to build a power supply that would run a 12V motor and supply both 3.3V and 1.85V. The 3.3V line must run an atmega328 microcontroller and a gyroscope. The 1.89V line must run a 3-axis acceleromter. That description probably gives the project away, but I’ll save the full write up for a future date. After lots of reading on the theory of power supply design and the problems that a dc motor can cause, I came up with the following design.

The power supply will get 12V in and first goes through a diode to protect the circuit (in case I some how put the batteries in backwards). Then it splits off 12V directly to the motor (which is controlled by an H bridge, I’ll cover that in a future post). After that, I put in a PI filter where the 100mH inductor to block out all (or most of) the ac frequency given out by the motor. The capacitors on either side help clean it up a bit further. The end result should be a steady dc out of the PI filter. Then the circuit splits off into two adjustable voltage regulators. By changing the voltage divider made by R1 and R2, the Vout on the regulator can be controlled. I calculated the appropriate resistor values, but got some pretty non-standard values, so I replaced R2 in each of the regulators with a 1Kohm potentiometer. With this change, I can just dial in the correct voltage. Each regulator has a protection diode to protect the regulator from the 1uF capacitor on the Vout side. Here it is set up on a breadboard. The Pi filter is on the upper portion of the board and the 1.85V regulator is on the lower portion. I still need to add in the 3.3V regulator.
